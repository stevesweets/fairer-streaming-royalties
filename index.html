Where does my streaming premium go?

What is this all about then?

I want to be clear. This is NOT a dig at spotify. I'm illustrating using their figures (or at least, an approximation of their figures), but this is about understanding how streaming royalties work (and why they are wrong). We focus too much on per-stream fees, and here's why.

This whole thing is predicated on the notion that to an artist, it shouldn't really be the focus that you want to be paid for every single occasion you listen to his music (after all, physical & digital sales are not time or play-limited). The artist does actually care if you listen to his music 10 times or 1000 times though, because they want to know that their music is appreciated. They care specifically, not for the number of plays, but for the appreciation and support that they personally gather.

Wait, wasn't there already something about this...?

Yep. Credit where it's due, I was informed on this subject by a few places - but initially it was this blog post by @cuepoint, I would suggest that you go and read it. If you have any energy left, come back.

You'll probably decry me at this stage and point out that sampling the top 100 tracks really doesn't get to the sum of the situation, and you'd be right to make that protest. That said, by looking at a smaller sample we're talking about multiples of pence, not pounds. If we sampled ALL listens, the split would be even less, fractions of pence. I'm actually being generous, by looking at such a small sample.

How can that be true?

I'm sure by now it's sunk in - what you personally listen to doesn't reflect where your money goes. I would suspect this is true even for those listeners with the most mainstream taste. Taylor Swift famously wrote an open letter to apple, and her focus was on her fans. Truth be told, only this would really help her dedicated fan base know that she was getting her money.

As @cuemusic discussed, splitting royalty money by listens AND person-by-person results in a much more satisfying result. The top artists would see less of a spike, but smaller artists with dedicated fanbases would see a MASSIVE increase in their royalty payouts. The top artists would be getting less, but would also be getting a more accurate payout.

I don't imagine this would go down well at all, but may well result in this big cultural shift at record labels.

tl;dr People you didn't listen to get your monies.

We focus on the artist, not the plays. If you decide to listen to Paul Simon's Graceland 1000 times this month, then little old paul gets all of your money.

tl;dr People you DID listen to should get your monies.

Afterthoughts

I do not believe Spotify to be in the wrong here. I believe it's taken a hard slog to get them where they are now, and I believe the time is coming fast when they will be able to call more shots on how they pay royalties - and I'd like to see them adopt a system like this.

I also don't think we should get bogged down into how much money ends up in the artists pocket, and how much goes to the label. Those particular figures will always be subject to their record deal. Record companies need a massive reform too, but if we achieve a fairer royalties system like this - then the likelihood is, many more of our favourite artists would be able to make real revenue from streaming. It may not be lots, but it would be an improvement. Suddenly, indie artists with small but dedicated fan bases could find themselves with a regular, albeit small income.

I am a spotify premium user, and love it. The service is great, flexible, and extensive. One might say that the best thing to do if I had a problem would be for me to stop using the service. I disagree. I like the service. I like it a lot, I discover a lot of new music because of it. I don't need a new service, and I'm sure spotify would agree. I would just like people to know exactly how royalties are paid, to make an informed decision on how they believe they *should* work.

It's possible that i've missed a great deal of information behind why it can't work any different, if that is the case, please feel free to blog reasons why.

I'm also presuming that Spotify retain 30% of all fees collected. I don't know this for sure, it could be any number. In fact, I don't have an issue with it even if it was 50%, it's their service I'm paying for after all.
